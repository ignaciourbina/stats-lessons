(S2-Q2) Summarizing and Graphing Numerical Data

To compute probabilities from a cumulative count table, divide each cohort’s count by the overall total, and do the same with cumulative counts for “up to” probabilities:

  • Probability of a specific cohort: P(21 to 30) = 600 / 3500 ≈ 0.171 (i.e. cohort size ÷ total population).
  • Cumulative probability “up to” a cohort: P(age ≤ 30) = 2100 / 3500 = 0.600 (i.e. cumulative count ÷ total).
  • General formulas:
    • P(\[a to b\]) = Population_{[a to b]} ÷ Total
    • P(age ≤ b) = CumulativeCount_{[0 to b]} ÷ Total

(S2-Q3) Summarizing and Graphing Numerical Data

Understanding a distribution’s shape—and its skewness—is crucial because it guides how we summarize data and anticipate unusual values:

  • Symmetric distributions (skew ≈ 0) look like mirror images on both sides; mean and median coincide, so the “average” truly represents a typical observation.
  • Right-skewed distributions (positive skew) have a long tail to the right; a few large values pull the mean above the median, warning us that extremes can inflate the average.
  • Left-skewed distributions (negative skew) have a long tail to the left; a handful of low values drag the mean below the median, alerting us that outliers may understate central tendency.
