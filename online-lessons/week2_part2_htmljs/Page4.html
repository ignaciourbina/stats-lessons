Tree Diagrams in Probability

Visualizing compound events using probability trees

Brief Introduction

Tree diagrams offer a powerful visual method for solving probability problems, particularly when dealing with sequential or compound events. By organizing outcomes and their probabilities in a branching structure, learners can easily track possible results and compute combined probabilities step by step.

Motivating Questions

How can we systematically list all possible outcomes of a multi-step event?
What rule helps us compute the probability of a sequence of events?
How do conditional probabilities affect the structure of a tree diagram?

Video Summary

This video introduces tree diagrams as a structured approach to visualize and calculate probabilities in multi-step experiments. Each branch in the tree represents a potential outcome and is labeled with the corresponding probability. The structure grows column by column as each stage of the experiment unfolds—for example, multiple coin tosses. A key principle is that probabilities branching from the same point must sum to one. To compute the likelihood of a particular path, the probabilities along that path are multiplied. An example using tourist visits to Big Ben and Trafalgar Square illustrates this with a final probability of 0.3. The video also explores how to verify a tree diagram's completeness by checking that all terminal probabilities sum to one and includes conditional probabilities, such as drawing without replacement. Viewers are invited to apply their understanding through a concluding practice problem.

In a probability tree diagram, the probabilities along all the branches that directly follow from the same point — that is, from the same split where an outcome occurs (like a coin toss or marble draw) — must always add up to 1.
